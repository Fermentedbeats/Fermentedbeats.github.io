Ruby: Keepin' it Class-y

Date: September 26, 2014

Tags: Dev Bootcamp, DBC, Ruby

Ruby classes are the Cadillacs of storage. Where arrays and hashes store data, and methods store actions, classes bring these abilities together along with the powerful option of creating and storing new objects. Let's check it out:



In this scenario, DBC is hosting a huge party to launch their brand of bootcamp vodka. Who wants to hire a bartender when we can code one?

Our initialize method kicks off our method by establishing two instance variables: "drink" and "like." The @ in front of the variable names declares them as instance variables, meaning these objects are available anywhere in the class.

Our instance method, make_drink, keeps tabs on our inventory and recipes, returning the appropriate drink to the customer when ordered. Let's check out a few calls:



In this example Martha moseys up to the bar and orders a vodka soda, and watches the magic happen.



Because we've accepted an optional argument, we're prepared for anything, including the customer who doesn't know what to order. When Martha returns, this time asking for something chocolatey, our Ruby-tender whips up a beverage, assigning a name to our drink variable:




Besides accessing swaths of info, classes act like a factory, letting you create new objects, and store them in a warehouse. To add inventory to the DBC bar, we create a new instance method that accepts a mixer, adding it to our set.



Now our boss can stroll through, order the house special, and stock the shelves:





Subsequent instance methods could be created to add more stock, recipes or even new products, but let's be honest, who is going to order anything with Clamato? We'll quit while we're ahead.
